AI Risk & Training 8 min read
Untrained on AI? Your Employees Are Already Making Costly Mistakes.
AI tools are now embedded in daily work across nearly every industry — but most organizations have deployed them without providing any structured training on how to use them correctly. The result is a growing pattern of errors, blind trust, and liability exposure that research is beginning to quantify in alarming detail.

What the Research Shows

  • Over-reliance is the #1 AI risk in the workplace. A 2024 Microsoft & LinkedIn Work Trend Index found that 78% of AI users bring their own tools to work — often without any organizational guidance — and that employees who over-trust AI output are significantly more likely to propagate errors without review. Source: Microsoft/LinkedIn Work Trend Index Annual Report, 2024
  • AI "hallucinations" are causing real-world errors. Stanford HAI research found that large language models produce confidently-stated but factually incorrect information in 3–27% of responses depending on the task domain — a rate most employees are completely unaware of. Source: Stanford Human-Centered AI (HAI) Institute, 2023–2024
  • Only 14% of employees have received formal AI training. Despite rapid adoption, a Salesforce survey of 14,000 workers found that just 1 in 7 have been given any structured guidance on AI use at work — leaving the vast majority to form their own (often incorrect) assumptions about accuracy and reliability. Source: Salesforce "Generative AI Snapshot Research Series," 2023
  • Errors from over-reliance cost organizations significantly. A 2023 Gartner report estimated that by 2025, poor-quality AI output — including unchecked AI-generated content used in business decisions — will cost organizations an average of $13 million per year in remediation and rework. Source: Gartner, "Innovation Insight: AI Trust, Risk and Security Management," 2023
  • Employees trained on AI ethics and verification make fewer errors. A University of Michigan study found that workers who received structured training on AI limitations and verification habits were 34% less likely to act on incorrect AI output compared to untrained peers. Source: University of Michigan School of Information, 2023

The Right Way to Train Employees on AI

Effective AI training isn't about telling employees to stop using it — it's about teaching them when to trust it, when to question it, and always how to verify it. Here's how WES approaches this:

  • AI Literacy First. Before any tool training, employees learn what AI actually is — including what it can and cannot do, how it generates output, and why confident-sounding answers are not the same as accurate ones.
  • Use-Case-Specific Guidance. WES builds training tailored to your tools and workflows — so employees learn the right way to use the specific AI features in their daily roles, not generic AI theory.
  • Verification Habits Built In. Every WES AI training module includes explicit checkpoints: when to fact-check, where to cross-reference, and how to recognize red flags in AI output before acting on it.
  • Ethical AI Use Frameworks. Employees learn how to handle sensitive data with AI, understand copyright and privacy boundaries, and apply your organization's acceptable-use policy — not just best guesses.
  • Ongoing Reinforcement. AI tools change fast. WES designs training that includes update cycles, so employees aren't learning on outdated assumptions as models evolve.

Know when to check. Know when to trust.

The biggest AI mistake isn't using it — it's using it without understanding its limits. WES teaches your team a simple, repeatable habit: use AI to accelerate, verify before you act, and never skip human judgment on high-stakes output. That one shift dramatically reduces errors, liability, and the costly rework that follows unchecked AI use.
